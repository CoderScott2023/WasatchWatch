NDVI Maps

How you can learn to read our maps

The Index

What is NDVI?

At Wasatch Watch, we use maps graded by an index known as NDVI. In order to understand how to read an NDVI map, you must understand what NDVI is.

NDVI, short for "normalized difference vegetation index" is an index that measures the relative greenness of an area. Essentially, how much vegetation cover there is in any one area of the plot relative to the rest of the plot. In this case, we use a relatively small-scale map of the Wasatch Front with 5 square km resolution (that is, each pixel you see in the graph is a 5 square km piece of land).

Value of 1.0 Most Vegetated Healthy, flourishing vegetation
Value of 0.2–0.3 Stressed Likely unhealthy vegetation
Value of 0.0 Unvegetated Roads, water bodies, bare soil

Map Type 2

NDVI Difference Maps

We also use a type of NDVI graph known as a difference graph. These difference graphs take the difference between the current month, and the month of comparison (typically the month of comparison will either be the previous month, or the same month in a different year). This shows a direct comparison of how the vegetation increased or decreased between the two time periods. A majority green difference graph means vegetation grew, while a majority red means vegetation was lost.

These graphs are labelled with the title "NDVI Difference Map", along with the date of the current month and the month of comparison in that order. An example is shown below:

Common Question

Why Does the Great Salt Lake Look Green?

Perhaps you have noticed the increase in vegetation over the Great Salt Lake in some difference maps. However, obviously the Great Salt Lake did not grow vegetation on it (or at least, not to such a significant degree). Because NDVI is a relative index, it appears that way. When the baseline of vegetation is less vegetated than normal, the Great Salt Lake appears more vegetated relative to a map where the vegetation baseline is much higher, despite being the same throughout the year. This also shows up as an artifact in the difference maps.

Methodological Note

This is a known artifact of relative NDVI normalization. Our newer reports use absolute raw NDVI values for difference maps, which eliminates this issue. Older reports may still show this effect.
